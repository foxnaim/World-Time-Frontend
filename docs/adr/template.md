## Context and Problem Statement

What is the forcing function? What constraints are we operating under?
What would happen if we did nothing? Two to four short paragraphs — enough
for a future reader who was not in the room to understand why this
decision had to be made at all.

State the problem as a question or a tension, not as the answer. If you
find yourself writing the decision here, stop and move it down.

## Considered Options

1. Option A — one-line summary.
2. Option B — one-line summary.
3. Option C — one-line summary.

List the options you seriously considered. A real ADR has at least two.
An ADR with only one option is documentation of a foregone conclusion,
not a decision.

## Decision

We adopt **Option X**.

Spell out what "Option X" actually means in concrete terms — components,
technologies, interfaces, policies. A reader should finish this section
knowing exactly what is in force.

Then, for each rejected option, state in one or two sentences _why_ it
was rejected. "We liked Option X more" is not a reason; "Option B
requires hardware we cannot deploy" is.

## Consequences

Positive:

- What do we get? What becomes easier?

Negative / tradeoffs:

- What did we give up? What becomes harder? Who might be unhappy?
- Be honest. An ADR with no tradeoffs is either about a trivial decision
  or is hiding something.

Neutral:

- Second-order effects, migration costs, follow-up decisions this
  implies, review triggers ("revisit if X happens").
